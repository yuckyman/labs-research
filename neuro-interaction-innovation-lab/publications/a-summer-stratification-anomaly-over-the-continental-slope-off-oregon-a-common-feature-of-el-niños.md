# A Summer Stratification Anomaly over the Continental Slope off Oregon: A Common Feature of El Niños?

## Abstract

<jats:title>Abstract</jats:title>
                  <jats:p>
                    Here, we verify if the effect of stratification weakening over the slope off Oregon (41°–46°N) is a feature of the recent major 2023 El Niño event, as well as the more distant 1997 event. Such an effect was linked recently to the arrival of El Niño–related coastally trapped wave (CTW) disturbances in summer 2014 and 2015 (Kurapov 2023). Regional ocean circulation model outputs are used to illustrate the responsible mechanism, which is caused by the anomalous poleward alongshore advection of the seasonal potential vorticity (PV) gradient. Analysis of the 1997–2023 time series of the conductivity–temperature–depth (CTD) profile data (275-m water depth) shows the strongly anomalous depression of the isopycnal surface 26.5 kg m
                    <jats:sup>−3</jats:sup>
                    by September 2023 and, simultaneously, the 80-m anomaly in the vertical separation between the isopycnal levels 26.25 and 26.5 kg m
                    <jats:sup>−3</jats:sup>
                    . This effect is similar to that observed in summer 2014 and 2015. However, the same effect is not present in the 1997 data, when both isopycnals under consideration deepened anomalously strongly, but their vertical separation was not anomalous. As a possible explanation, the southward, upwelling favorable wind was anomalously weak over most of the upwelling season in 1997, while the wind conditions were closer to average in 2014, 2015, and 2023. The discussion of the slope anomalies is augmented by the analysis of a multiyear series of the acoustic Doppler current profiler (ADCP) horizontal velocity data on the shelf (81-m water depth). In August–September 2023 and in June 2015, when the slope PV anomaly grew, the shelf currents showed anomalous behavior.
                  </jats:p>
                  <jats:sec>
                    <jats:title>Significance Statement</jats:title>
                    <jats:p>El Niño is a global ocean–atmosphere anomaly pattern that originates in the equatorial Pacific and impacts oceanic currents and density along the U.S. West Coast. This impact is similar between the most recent 2023 El Niño and the earlier 2014–15 El Niño, as confirmed by the analysis of the water density and velocity data on the shelf and continental slope off Oregon. El Niño acts to aid water mass displacement along the slope from south to north. Potential vorticity is a quantity related to the density stratification that may be used as an indicator of the arrival of the oceanic El Niño signal at the Oregon coast.</jats:p>
                  </jats:sec>
